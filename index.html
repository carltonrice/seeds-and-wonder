Morning Outdoor Window

🦁 Backyard Animal Safari

20–25 min · Science + Gross Motor Before 10:30am
›

What You'll Need

  • Animal toys from the toy bin (the ones they already have)
  • A bucket or basket for "catching" animals
  • Blanket or towels for habitats (blue = water, green = jungle, brown = desert)
  • Binoculars (real or made from toilet paper rolls)

How To Do It

Before the boys wake up (or while they eat breakfast), hide 8–10 animal toys around the backyard. Under bushes, on the porch rail, tucked in a planter, peeking from behind a chair.

Give each boy a basket: "We're going on a SAFARI!" Walk the yard together. When they spot one: "What animal is that? What sound does it make? Where does it live?" Keep it moving so you're back inside before the heat builds.

Tie to faith: "God made SO many animals. Let's see how many we can find that He made!"

🦖 Logan (3y3m) He leads the safari. Give him the binoculars. Ask: "Is that a jungle animal or an ocean animal?" Have him stomp like an elephant when he finds one, slither like a snake, hop like a frog. Count your total finds together.
🐻 Caleb (1y6m) He toddles along and picks up whatever he finds. Name every animal loudly: "LION! Rawr!" Let him drop them in the bucket. The find-and-drop is the whole game for him. Celebrate every discovery.
Walking Through the Jungle — Barefoot Books
Sing-along to get them in safari mode before you go out
🔄 Alternatives
  • Too warm already? Do the safari indoors. Hide animals around the living room.
  • No binoculars? Tape two toilet paper tubes together. Logan will love making them.
  • Extra challenge (Logan): Give him a "safari checklist" with animal pictures to check off as he finds them.
  • Real animals: While outside, look for real bugs, birds, or lizards too.

Indoor Activities (Midday)

🌍 Animal Habitat Sorting

15–20 min · Science + Math Midday indoor
›

What You'll Need

  • All the animal toys from the safari (bring them inside)
  • 3 containers or sections of floor: jungle, ocean, farm
  • Blue towel or construction paper = ocean, green = jungle, brown paper bag or tan towel = farm/desert
  • Optional: blocks or Duplos to build "homes"

How To Do It

Lay out three "habitats" on the floor. Hold up each animal from the safari basket: "Where does the lion live? Jungle or ocean?" Place it in the right zone. Build up each habitat with blocks, pillows, or whatever you have.

Count how many live in each place. "The jungle has the MOST!" Make animal sounds as you sort. This is the calm-down transition from the morning outdoor energy.

🦖 Logan (3y3m) He decides where each animal goes. Ask "why" questions: "Why does the fish live in the ocean?" Let him build enclosures with blocks. If he wants a dinosaur habitat, let him create one. Count and compare groups: "Which habitat has more?"
🐻 Caleb (1y6m) Hand him animals one at a time. Name them. Let him drop them wherever he wants (process over accuracy). He'll love the texture of the towels and the stacking of blocks. Repeat animal sounds: "Mooo! That's the cow!"
Animal Habitats for Kids — Peekaboo Kidz
Colorful 5-min explainer, perfect background while sorting
🔄 Alternatives
  • No towels? Draw habitat zones on a big piece of paper or use plates
  • Fewer animals? Use pictures from a magazine or print animal photos
  • Extension (Logan): After sorting, draw each habitat and the animals that live there

🚢 Noah's Ark Animal Parade

15–20 min · Faith + Math + Movement Midday indoor
›

What You'll Need

  • Animal toys (same ones from safari and sorting)
  • A big box, laundry basket, or couch cushion "boat"
  • Optional: The Beginner's Bible Noah's Ark story

How To Do It

Tell the story simply: "God told Noah to build a big boat and bring two of every animal. Let's help!" Set up the "ark" (a box or basket) in the middle of the room.

March the animals in two by two. Count as they board: "Two lions! Two elephants!" Make each animal's sound as it walks on. Logan and Caleb ARE the parade, marching and stomping around the room carrying animals to the ark.

"God kept every animal safe. He keeps US safe too."

🦖 Logan (3y3m) He's the parade leader. He picks which animals go next. Practice counting by twos: "Two, four, six!" Ask: "Which animals are the biggest? They go on the bottom of the boat." Let him arrange them inside the ark.
🐻 Caleb (1y6m) He marches. He stomps. He carries one animal at a time to the ark and drops it in. The in-and-out of the basket is the game. Clap and cheer each delivery: "Caleb brought the GIRAFFE!"
The Animals Went in Two by Two — Nursery Rhyme
Sing along during the parade
🔄 Alternatives
  • No big box? Use a laundry basket, bathtub, or tape an outline on the floor
  • Quiet version: Skip the marching. Just line up pairs on the couch "boat" while reading the story
  • Extension: After loading the ark, make rain sounds (bang on pots!) and then "open the window" to let the rainbow in

📖 "Dear Zoo" Read-Aloud

10–15 min · Language + Sensory Midday wind-down
›

What You'll Need

  • Dear Zoo by Rod Campbell (or any animal book with flaps)
  • Cozy spot: couch, floor blanket, or Caleb's room
  • The matching animal toys for each page (if you have them)

How To Do It

Read together. Before lifting each flap, ask: "What do you think the zoo sent THIS time?" Let them guess. When you reveal the animal, find the matching toy and hold it up. Make the sound together.

This is the calm-down activity. Low energy, cozy, snuggly. Perfect for after the parade when everyone needs to recharge before the afternoon.

🦖 Logan (3y3m) He lifts the flaps. He guesses. Ask: "Why did they send him back?" (too big, too tall, too grumpy). He'll memorize the pattern fast: "I know! It's too SCARY!" Let him "read" it to Caleb.
🐻 Caleb (1y6m) Flap books are magic at this age. He lifts, he sees, he points. Name everything. Let him hold the matching animal toy while you read. If he wanders after 5 minutes, that's fine. Lap time counts.

  • Don't have this book? Brown Bear Brown Bear, From Head to Toe by Eric Carle, or any animal book
  • No book at all? Use the YouTube read-aloud above, or just play "What animal am I?" with sounds and let them guess
  • Extension: After reading, each person picks their favorite animal from the book and acts it out

Late Afternoon Outdoor Window

🌿 Bug & Bird Watch

15–20 min · Science + Nature + Faith After 4pm
›

What You'll Need

  • Binoculars from the morning (or just your eyes)
  • A magnifying glass (or phone camera zoomed in)
  • Small jar or container for temporary bug observation
  • Sidewalk chalk for drawing what you find

How To Do It

Head back outside once it cools down (after 4pm, partly sunny by then). This time: look for REAL animals. Ants on the sidewalk, birds in the trees, rollie pollies under rocks, maybe a lizard on the fence.

Sit in the grass together and just watch. "How many birds can you count?" Gently catch a bug in the jar, look at it, let it go. Draw what you found with sidewalk chalk on the driveway.

End the day: "We played with our toy animals AND found real ones. God made them ALL. Even the tiny bugs. Even the birds way up high."

🦖 Logan (3y3m) He's the lead naturalist. Count ants. Watch where the bird flies. "What do you think that bug eats?" Let him hold the magnifying glass. Draw his favorite animal on the driveway with chalk. Compare: "Is the real ant bigger or smaller than your toy ant?"
🐻 Caleb (1y6m) Grass, dirt, rocks, leaves. He's in sensory heaven just being outside. Point at birds: "BIRD! Up there!" Let him hold the chalk and scribble. If he finds an ant, narrate: "Tiny! So tiny! He's walking!" Keep him close for bug safety.
Bugs Bugs Bugs! — The Kiboomers
Fun insect song to play on the way outside
🔄 Alternatives
  • Still too hot at 4pm? Do a window watch from inside. Set up near a window and count birds/squirrels.
  • No bugs out? Water a patch of dirt and wait 5 minutes. Bugs will come.
  • Extension: Take photos of every animal you find. Make a "field guide" later.
  • Evening walk: Take the safari to the neighborhood. Walk the block and count every animal (dogs, cats, birds, squirrels).
